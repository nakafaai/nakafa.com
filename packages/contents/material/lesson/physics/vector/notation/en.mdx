## Arrows as Vector Language

A vector is commonly drawn as an arrow. The drawing is not decorative. The tail shows the starting point, the tip shows the direction, and the arrow length represents the vector magnitude.

If a force arrow is longer, the force is larger on the same drawing scale. If the arrow points up and to the right, the force acts up and to the right.

> Vector notation should keep two pieces of information visible: magnitude and direction.

## Two-Point Notation

A vector can be written from a starting point to an ending point. For example, <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{AB}" /> means the vector from point <InlineMath math="A" /> to point <InlineMath math="B" />.

The order of the letters matters. <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{AB}" /> and <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{BA}" /> use the same two points, but their directions are opposite.

| Notation | How to read it | Direction meaning |
| :------- | :------------- | :---------------- |
| <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{AB}" /> | vector from <InlineMath math="A" /> to <InlineMath math="B" /> | tail at <InlineMath math="A" />, tip at <InlineMath math="B" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{BA}" /> | vector from <InlineMath math="B" /> to <InlineMath math="A" /> | tail at <InlineMath math="B" />, tip at <InlineMath math="A" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\lvert\overrightarrow{AB}\rvert" /> | magnitude of <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{AB}" /> | length only, not direction |

The arrow above the letters tells us that the quantity is a vector. Absolute-value bars tell us that only the magnitude is being discussed.

## Single-Letter Notation

In calculations, vectors are often named with one letter such as <InlineMath math="\vec{a}" />, <InlineMath math="\vec{v}" />, or <InlineMath math="\vec{F}" />. The letter helps identify the physical quantity.

For example, <InlineMath math="\vec{F}" /> is often used for force, <InlineMath math="\vec{v}" /> for velocity, and <InlineMath math="\vec{s}" /> for displacement. The magnitude is written without the arrow, such as <InlineMath math="F" /> or <InlineMath math="\lvert\vec{F}\rvert" />.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\vec{F}=20\text{ N to the right}" />
  <BlockMath math="\lvert\vec{F}\rvert=20\text{ N}" />
</MathContainer>

The first line still includes direction. The second line states only the force magnitude.

## Zero Vectors and Unit Vectors

Two special vector names appear often once vectors are calculated using components.

A **zero vector** has magnitude <InlineMath math="0" />. Its tail and tip coincide, so its direction is undefined. In physics, a zero vector can appear when two equal and opposite forces cancel each other.

A **unit vector** has magnitude <InlineMath math="1" /> and only shows direction. Unit vectors do not tell us how strong a force is or how far a displacement is. They identify axis directions.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\hat{i}=\text{unit vector in the }x\text{ direction}" />
  <BlockMath math="\hat{j}=\text{unit vector in the }y\text{ direction}" />
  <BlockMath math="\hat{k}=\text{unit vector in the }z\text{ direction}" />
</MathContainer>

Later, when a vector is written as <InlineMath math="\vec{A}=A_x\hat{i}+A_y\hat{j}" />, <InlineMath math="\hat{i}" /> and <InlineMath math="\hat{j}" /> give the directions, while <InlineMath math="A_x" /> and <InlineMath math="A_y" /> give the component magnitudes along each axis.

## Common Reading Mistakes

The first mistake is treating <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{AB}" /> as the same vector as <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{BA}" /> because they use the same two points. Their directions are opposite.

The second mistake is writing a vector magnitude as a negative number. A magnitude is always <InlineMath math="0" /> or positive. A negative sign on a vector usually means the direction is opposite to a chosen reference direction, not that the length is negative.

For example, <InlineMath math="-\vec{a}" /> does not mean the vector has negative magnitude. It means the vector has the same magnitude as <InlineMath math="\vec{a}" /> but points in the opposite direction.
