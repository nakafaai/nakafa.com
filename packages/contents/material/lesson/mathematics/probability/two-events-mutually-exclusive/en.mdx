## What Does Mutually Exclusive Mean?

Imagine you have two choices, but you can only pick **one**, not both at the same time. Well, in the world of probability, this is similar to the concept of **Mutually Exclusive Events** (other cool names: _Disjoint Events_).

Two events (let's call them event <InlineMath math="A" /> and event <InlineMath math="B" />) are said to be **mutually exclusive** if both events **cannot possibly occur at the same time** in a single trial. Simply put, if <InlineMath math="A" /> happens, <InlineMath math="B" /> cannot happen. If <InlineMath math="B" /> happens, <InlineMath math="A" /> cannot happen.

## Characteristics of Mutually Exclusive Events

The main characteristic is just that: **cannot happen simultaneously**. There's no outcome that can belong to event <InlineMath math="A" /> and event <InlineMath math="B" /> at the same time.

### Probability of Two Events Occurring Together

Because events <InlineMath math="A" /> and <InlineMath math="B" /> cannot happen together if they are mutually exclusive, the probability of both occurring simultaneously is **zero**!

We can write the probability of event "<InlineMath math="A" /> **and** <InlineMath math="B" />" (both occurring) as:

<BlockMath math="P(A \text{ and} B) = 0" />

Or using the intersection symbol:

<BlockMath math="P(A \cap B) = 0" />

Remember, if they are mutually exclusive, their intersection is empty, so the probability is zero!

## Calculating the Combined Probability for Mutually Exclusive Events

So, how do we calculate the probability of event <InlineMath math="A" /> happening **OR** event <InlineMath math="B" /> happening if <InlineMath math="A" /> and <InlineMath math="B" /> are mutually exclusive?

Since they can't happen together, we simply **add** the probabilities of each individual event.

The formula becomes very easy:

<BlockMath math="P(A \text{ or} B) = P(A) + P(B)" />

Or using the union symbol:

<BlockMath math="P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)" />

This is the **Special Addition Rule** which applies ONLY to mutually exclusive events. (If the events are not mutually exclusive, there's a slightly different formula).

## Examples of Mutually Exclusive Events

To understand better, look at these examples:

1.  **Coin Toss:**

    The event of getting "Heads" and the event of getting "Tails". They can't happen together, right?

    - <InlineMath math="P(\text{Heads}) = 1/2" />
    - <InlineMath math="P(\text{Tails}) = 1/2" />
    - Probability of getting Heads OR Tails is <InlineMath math="P(\text{Heads}) + P(\text{Tails}) = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1" /> (One of them must occur).

2.  **Rolling a Die (once):**

    Look at two pairs of events:

    - Getting a <InlineMath math="3" /> and getting a <InlineMath math="5" /> cannot happen at once. Each has probability <InlineMath math="1/6" />, so the probability of getting a <InlineMath math="3" /> or <InlineMath math="5" /> is <InlineMath math="1/6 + 1/6 = 2/6 = 1/3" />.
    - Getting an even number <InlineMath math="\{2, 4, 6\}" /> and getting an odd number <InlineMath math="\{1, 3, 5\}" /> cannot happen at once. Each event has probability <InlineMath math="3/6 = 1/2" />, so the probability of getting even or odd is <InlineMath math="1/2 + 1/2 = 1" />.

3.  **Drawing a Card (once):**

    Use the same idea for card events:

    - Getting a King and getting a Queen are mutually exclusive. There are <InlineMath math="4" /> Kings and <InlineMath math="4" /> Queens in <InlineMath math="52 \text{ cards}" />, so the probability of getting a King or Queen is <InlineMath math="4/52 + 4/52 = 8/52 = 2/13" />.
    - Getting a red card and getting a club <InlineMath math="\clubsuit" /> are mutually exclusive because clubs are black. There are <InlineMath math="26" /> red cards and <InlineMath math="13" /> clubs, so the probability of getting red or club is <InlineMath math="26/52 + 13/52 = 39/52 = 3/4" />.
