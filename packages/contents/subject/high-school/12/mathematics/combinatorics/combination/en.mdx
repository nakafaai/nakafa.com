## Understanding Combination

Imagine you are asked to choose <InlineMath math="3 \text{ friends}" /> to join a futsal team. Does the order of selection matter? Of course not! What matters is **who** is selected, not the order in which they are chosen.

This is the fundamental difference between combination and permutation. **Combination** is a way to select a certain number of objects from a larger collection of objects, where **order is not considered**.

In daily life, we often encounter combinations when:
- Choosing food menus from a list of options
- Determining team members for an activity
- Selecting elective subjects at school
- Determining color combinations for design

The difference from permutation is very clear: if in permutation <InlineMath math="ABC" /> is different from <InlineMath math="BAC" />, then in combination <InlineMath math="ABC" /> is the same as <InlineMath math="BAC" /> because the members are the same, only the order is different.

## Combination Formula

To determine the number of ways to select <InlineMath math="k" /> objects from <InlineMath math="n" /> available objects, we use the combination formula:

<BlockMath math="C(n,k) = \binom{n}{k} = \frac{n!}{(n-k)! \times k!}" />

Where:
- <InlineMath math="C(n,k)" /> or <InlineMath math="\binom{n}{k}" /> = combination of <InlineMath math="k" /> objects from <InlineMath math="n" /> objects
- <InlineMath math="n" /> = total available objects
- <InlineMath math="k" /> = number of objects to be selected
- <InlineMath math="n!" /> = factorial of <InlineMath math="n" />

**Why is this formula different from permutation?**

The combination formula actually comes from the permutation formula divided by <InlineMath math="k!" />:

<BlockMath math="C(n,k) = \frac{P(n,k)}{k!} = \frac{n!}{(n-k)! \times k!}" />

Division by <InlineMath math="k!" /> is done because in combinations, we **don't care about order**. Each group of <InlineMath math="k" /> objects has <InlineMath math="k!" /> different arrangement possibilities, but they are all considered **the same** in combinations.

## Applications in Real Situations

**Sports Team Formation:**

From <InlineMath math="10 \text{ available students}" />, how many ways can we select <InlineMath math="5 \text{ students}" /> for a basketball team?

<MathContainer>
<BlockMath math="C(10,5) = \frac{10!}{5! \times 5!}" />
<BlockMath math="C(10,5) = \frac{10 \times 9 \times 8 \times 7 \times 6}{5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{30.240}{120} = 252 \text{ ways}" />
</MathContainer>

**Food Menu Selection:**

A restaurant offers <InlineMath math="8 \text{ types of food}" /> and you can choose <InlineMath math="3 \text{ types}" />. How many possible combination choices are there?

<MathContainer>
<BlockMath math="C(8,3) = \frac{8!}{5! \times 3!}" />
<BlockMath math="C(8,3) = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{336}{6} = 56 \text{ combinations}" />
</MathContainer>

**Situations with Special Conditions:**

Sometimes there are **certain restrictions** in selection. For example, from <InlineMath math="12 \text{ students}" /> (<InlineMath math="7 \text{ males}" /> and <InlineMath math="5 \text{ females}" />), we must select <InlineMath math="4 \text{ students}" /> with the condition of at least <InlineMath math="2 \text{ females}" />.

**Solution strategy:**
1. **Count all possibilities** that meet the conditions
2. **Separate based on conditions:** <InlineMath math="2 \text{ females} + 2 \text{ males}" />, <InlineMath math="3 \text{ females} + 1 \text{ male}" />, or <InlineMath math="4 \text{ females}" />
3. **Sum** all possibilities

**Detailed calculations:**

**Case** <InlineMath math="1" />: <InlineMath math="2 \text{ females} + 2 \text{ males}" />

<MathContainer>
<BlockMath math="C(5,2) \times C(7,2) = \frac{5!}{3! \times 2!} \times \frac{7!}{5! \times 2!}" />
<BlockMath math="= \frac{5 \times 4}{2 \times 1} \times \frac{7 \times 6}{2 \times 1} = 10 \times 21 = 210" />
</MathContainer>

**Case** <InlineMath math="2" />: <InlineMath math="3 \text{ females} + 1 \text{ male}" />

<MathContainer>
<BlockMath math="C(5,3) \times C(7,1) = \frac{5!}{2! \times 3!} \times \frac{7!}{6! \times 1!}" />
<BlockMath math="= \frac{5 \times 4}{2 \times 1} \times 7 = 10 \times 7 = 70" />
</MathContainer>

**Case** <InlineMath math="3" />: <InlineMath math="4 \text{ females} + 0 \text{ males}" />

<MathContainer>
<BlockMath math="C(5,4) = \frac{5!}{1! \times 4!} = \frac{5}{1} = 5" />
</MathContainer>

**Total:** <InlineMath math="210 + 70 + 5 = 285" /> ways

## Practice Problems

1. From <InlineMath math="8 \text{ different books}" />, how many ways can you choose <InlineMath math="3 \text{ books}" /> to read during vacation?

2. A futsal team has <InlineMath math="12 \text{ players}" />. How many ways can they select <InlineMath math="5 \text{ players}" /> to play on the field?

3. In a box there are <InlineMath math="6 \text{ red balls}" /> and <InlineMath math="4 \text{ blue balls}" />. How many ways can you take <InlineMath math="5 \text{ balls}" /> with the condition of at least <InlineMath math="3 \text{ red balls}" />?

4. A student must choose <InlineMath math="4 \text{ subjects}" /> from <InlineMath math="10 \text{ available subjects}" />. If <InlineMath math="6 \text{ subjects}" /> are mandatory and <InlineMath math="4 \text{ subjects}" /> are elective, how many ways can they choose if there must be at least <InlineMath math="2 \text{ mandatory subjects}" />?

### Answer Key

1. **Answer: <InlineMath math="56 \text{ ways}" />**
   
   Solution steps:
   
   Given: <InlineMath math="n = 8" /> books, select <InlineMath math="k = 3" /> books
   
   Combination formula:
   
   <MathContainer>
      <BlockMath math="C(n,k) = \frac{n!}{(n-k)! \times k!}" />
      <BlockMath math="C(8,3) = \frac{8!}{5! \times 3!} = \frac{8 \times 7 \times 6}{3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{336}{6} = 56" />
   </MathContainer>
   
   Therefore, there are <InlineMath math="56 \text{ ways}" /> to choose <InlineMath math="3 \text{ books}" /> from <InlineMath math="8 \text{ available books}" />.

2. **Answer: <InlineMath math="792 \text{ ways}" />**
   
   Solution steps:
   
   Given: <InlineMath math="n = 12" /> players, select <InlineMath math="k = 5" /> players
   
   Combination formula:
   
   <MathContainer>
      <BlockMath math="C(12,5) = \frac{12!}{7! \times 5!}" />
      <BlockMath math="C(12,5) = \frac{12 \times 11 \times 10 \times 9 \times 8}{5 \times 4 \times 3 \times 2 \times 1} = \frac{95.040}{120} = 792" />
   </MathContainer>
   
   The futsal team can select <InlineMath math="5 \text{ players}" /> from <InlineMath math="12 \text{ players}" /> in <InlineMath math="792 \text{ different ways}" />.

3. **Answer: <InlineMath math="186 \text{ ways}" />**
   
   Solution steps (**case method**):
   
   Total balls: <InlineMath math="6 \text{ red} + 4 \text{ blue}" /> is <InlineMath math="10 \text{ balls}" />
   
   Take <InlineMath math="5 \text{ balls}" /> with at least <InlineMath math="3" /> red balls
   
   **Detailed calculation for each case:**
   
   **Case** <InlineMath math="1" />: <InlineMath math="3 \text{ red} + 2 \text{ blue}" />

   <MathContainer>
   <BlockMath math="C(6,3) \times C(4,2) = \frac{6!}{3! \times 3!} \times \frac{4!}{2! \times 2!}" />
   <BlockMath math="= \frac{6 \times 5 \times 4}{3 \times 2 \times 1} \times \frac{4 \times 3}{2 \times 1} = 20 \times 6 = 120" />
   </MathContainer>
   
   **Case** <InlineMath math="2" />: <InlineMath math="4 \text{ red} + 1 \text{ blue}" />

   <MathContainer>
   <BlockMath math="C(6,4) \times C(4,1) = \frac{6!}{2! \times 4!} \times \frac{4!}{3! \times 1!}" />
   <BlockMath math="= \frac{6 \times 5}{2 \times 1} \times 4 = 15 \times 4 = 60" />
   </MathContainer>
   
   **Case** <InlineMath math="3" />: <InlineMath math="5 \text{ red} + 0 \text{ blue}" />

   <MathContainer>
   <BlockMath math="C(6,5) \times C(4,0) = \frac{6!}{1! \times 5!} \times \frac{4!}{4! \times 0!}" />
   <BlockMath math="= 6 \times 1 = 6" />
   </MathContainer>
   
   **Total:** <InlineMath math="120 + 60 + 6 = 186" />
   
   There are <InlineMath math="186 \text{ ways}" /> to take <InlineMath math="5 \text{ balls}" /> with at least <InlineMath math="3" /> red balls.

4. **Answer: <InlineMath math="185 \text{ ways}" />**
   
   Solution steps (**case method**):
   
   Mandatory subjects: <InlineMath math="6" />, elective subjects: <InlineMath math="4" />
