## Shells as Energy Levels

In the simple Bohr model, electrons are shown in **atomic shells**, which represent specific energy levels around the nucleus. A shell closer to the nucleus has lower energy. A shell farther from the nucleus has higher energy.

OpenStax explains that the Bohr model uses orbits with specific energies to explain the line spectrum of hydrogen. The reference is available at [The Bohr Model from OpenStax](https://openstax.org/books/chemistry-atoms-first-2e/pages/3-2-the-bohr-model).

Picture a building with floors. The first floor is near the ground, the second floor is higher, and so on. In an atom, the "floor" is not a physical floor. It is an energy level. So, a circular shell diagram is an **energy-level sketch**, not a rigid track that electrons follow like a train.

Shells are named with letters:

| Shell | Shell number |
| :---- | :----------- |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{K}" /> | <InlineMath math="n = 1" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{L}" /> | <InlineMath math="n = 2" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> | <InlineMath math="n = 3" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{N}" /> | <InlineMath math="n = 4" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{O}" /> | <InlineMath math="n = 5" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{P}" /> | <InlineMath math="n = 6" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Q}" /> | <InlineMath math="n = 7" /> |

The letters <InlineMath math="\mathrm{K}" /> through <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Q}" /> are shell names. The value <InlineMath math="n" /> is the **shell number**. A larger <InlineMath math="n" /> means a farther energy level.

## Maximum Electron Capacity

The maximum number of electrons in one shell is calculated with:

<BlockMath math="\text{maximum electrons in a shell} = 2n^2" />

LibreTexts explains the shell names <InlineMath math="\mathrm{K}" /> through <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Q}" /> and their maximum capacities in its Bohr model discussion. The reference is available at [The Bohr Model of Atoms from LibreTexts](https://chem.libretexts.org/Courses/Barstow_Community_College/Survey_of_Chemistry_and_Physics/02%3A_Structure_of_Matter/2.03%3A_Atomic_Theory/2.3.01%3A_Atomic_Structure/2.3.1.01%3A_The_Bohr_Model_of_Atoms).

The results are:

| Shell | Shell number | Maximum electrons from <InlineMath math="2n^2" /> |
| :---- | :----------- | :----------------------------------------------- |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{K}" /> | <InlineMath math="1" /> | <InlineMath math="2(1)^2 = 2" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{L}" /> | <InlineMath math="2" /> | <InlineMath math="2(2)^2 = 8" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> | <InlineMath math="3" /> | <InlineMath math="2(3)^2 = 18" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{N}" /> | <InlineMath math="4" /> | <InlineMath math="2(4)^2 = 32" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{O}" /> | <InlineMath math="5" /> | <InlineMath math="2(5)^2 = 50" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{P}" /> | <InlineMath math="6" /> | <InlineMath math="2(6)^2 = 72" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Q}" /> | <InlineMath math="7" /> | <InlineMath math="2(7)^2 = 98" /> |

The word **maximum** matters. Shell <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> can hold up to <InlineMath math="18" /> electrons, but that does not mean every atom fills <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> to <InlineMath math="18" /> before using the next shell. For introductory examples up to calcium, we use the simpler pattern in the interactive lab.

## Why Calcium Is Two Eight Eight Two

Calcium has atomic number <InlineMath math="20" />. The symbol <InlineMath math="Z" /> means atomic number. Since the atom is neutral, it also has <InlineMath math="20" /> electrons.

### Calcium Shell Filling Steps

The filling steps in the simple shell model are:

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
\mathrm{Ca}: Z &= 20 \\
e^- &= 20 \\
\mathrm{K} &: 2 \quad \text{left } 18 \\
\mathrm{L} &: 8 \quad \text{left } 10 \\
\mathrm{M} &: 8 \quad \text{left } 2 \\
\mathrm{N} &: 2 \quad \text{left } 0
\end{aligned}" />

### Why Not Two Eight Ten

A common question is: why not <InlineMath math="2, 8, 10" />?

The answer is that in the simple shell model for light elements up to calcium, shell <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> is written with <InlineMath math="8" /> electrons before the remaining electrons begin shell <InlineMath math="\mathrm{N}" />. After shells <InlineMath math="\mathrm{K}" /> and <InlineMath math="\mathrm{L}" /> are filled, calcium has <InlineMath math="10" /> electrons left, so shell <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> is written as <InlineMath math="8" /> and shell <InlineMath math="\mathrm{N}" /> receives the remaining <InlineMath math="2" />.

Therefore:

<BlockMath math="\text{calcium electron configuration} = 2, 8, 8, 2" />

## Limits of the Model

Do not mix up these two ideas.

| Statement | Accurate meaning |
| :-------- | :--------------- |
| Atomic shells are drawn as circles. | The drawing helps show energy levels. It is not a rigid physical path. |
| The formula <InlineMath math="2n^2" /> gives maximum capacity. | It is not the complete filling order for every element. Heavier elements need the subshell and orbital model. |

OpenStax explains that in the modern orbital model, electrons after argon enter the <InlineMath math="4s" /> level before <InlineMath math="3d" />. The reference is available at [Electronic Structure of Atoms from OpenStax](https://openstax.org/books/chemistry/pages/6-4-electronic-structure-of-atoms-electron-configurations). Here, we stop at the simple shell pattern so the foundation stays clear.

The outermost shell also leads into the idea of valence electrons. Electrons in the outermost shell are called **valence electrons**, and they strongly affect whether an atom tends to form ions or chemical bonds.
