## Three Properties to Read

We already know the three particles that make up atoms: **protons**, **neutrons**, and **electrons**. Now the focus is not “who discovered them,” but **which properties make them different**.

Three properties matter most in chemistry class:

- **charge**, the electric sign of the particle
- **mass**, how much the particle contributes to atomic mass
- **location**, the part of the atom where the particle is usually discussed

The Atomic Structure and Symbolism section from OpenStax gives a table of subatomic particle charge, mass, and location. The source table can be opened through [OpenStax's Properties of Subatomic Particles](https://openstax.org/books/chemistry-atoms-first-2e/pages/2-3-atomic-structure-and-symbolism).

For more precise constants, NIST publishes CODATA recommended values. The summary can be opened through [NIST's CODATA 2022 wall chart](https://physics.nist.gov/cuu/pdf/wall_2022.pdf).

## Charge Decides the Direction of Bending

Electric charge can be read as a particle's “interaction sign.” A proton is positive, an electron is negative, and a neutron has no total charge.

The proton and electron have the same charge magnitude, but opposite signs:

<BlockMath math="e = 1.602176634 \times 10^{-19}\ \text{C}" />

That means:

- a proton has charge <InlineMath math="+e" />
- an electron has charge <InlineMath math="-e" />
- a neutron has charge <InlineMath math="0" />

In an electric field, a charged particle feels a force. The short way to read it is:

<BlockMath math="F = qE" />

The symbol <InlineMath math="q" /> is the particle charge, and <InlineMath math="E" /> is the electric field. If <InlineMath math="q" /> is positive, the force points with the field. If <InlineMath math="q" /> is negative, the force points the opposite way. If <InlineMath math="q = 0" />, the particle is not bent by a simple electric field.

A quick analogy: an electric field is like wind, but only objects with charge can “feel” it. Protons and electrons both feel the push, but in opposite directions. A neutron is neutral, so in this class-level model it goes straight.

## Mass Changes How Strongly Motion Changes

Subatomic particle masses are tiny, so we often use the **unified atomic mass unit**, written <InlineMath math="\text{u}" />. It is defined from the mass of a carbon-12 atom.

| Particle | Relative charge | Relative mass | Absolute mass | Main location |
| :------- | :-------------- | :------------ | :------------ | :------------ |
| Proton | <InlineMath math="+1" /> | <InlineMath math="1.0073\ \text{u}" /> | <InlineMath math="1.67262 \times 10^{-24}\ \text{g}" /> | Nucleus |
| Neutron | <InlineMath math="0" /> | <InlineMath math="1.0087\ \text{u}" /> | <InlineMath math="1.67493 \times 10^{-24}\ \text{g}" /> | Nucleus |
| Electron | <InlineMath math="-1" /> | <InlineMath math="0.00055\ \text{u}" /> | <InlineMath math="9.10939 \times 10^{-28}\ \text{g}" /> | Space around the nucleus |

The table shows two important ideas. First, protons and neutrons are almost equally heavy. Second, electrons are extremely light compared with protons and neutrons.

A simple comparison is:

<BlockMath math="\frac{m_p}{m_e} \approx 1836" />

So one proton has about the same mass as <InlineMath math="1836" /> electrons. That is why almost all atomic mass comes from the nucleus, not from the electrons.

Imagine pushing two objects with the same force: a tennis ball changes motion more easily than a bowling ball. This is only a mass analogy, not the shape of a particle. In an atom, an electron is much lighter, so a force can change its motion much more than it changes a proton's motion.

## Location Gives Each Particle Its Role

Particle location gives each particle a different role.

| Particle | Location | Common role in chemistry |
| :------- | :------- | :----------------------- |
| Proton | Nucleus | Determines element identity. |
| Neutron | Nucleus | Adds mass and distinguishes isotopes. |
| Electron | Space around the nucleus | Strongly affects reactions and ion formation. |

The number of protons determines the element. For example, an atom with <InlineMath math="6" /> protons is carbon. If the neutron number changes, the element is still carbon, but the isotope is different. If the electron number changes, the atom becomes an ion.

For a neutral atom, the number of protons and electrons is the same:

<BlockMath math="\text{neutral atom} \Rightarrow p^+ = e^-" />

That sentence does not mean protons and electrons have the same mass. It means the total positive and negative charges balance.

## Ready for Atomic Symbols

In the next subchapter, atomic symbols will use three important numbers. Subatomic particle properties tell us how to read them:

- the number of protons is read as the **atomic number**
- the number of protons plus neutrons is read as the **mass number**
- the difference between protons and electrons is read as the **ion charge**

So do not memorize the table as disconnected numbers. Read the main relationship: **protons give identity**, **neutrons add mass**, and **electrons control the atom's outer charge**.
