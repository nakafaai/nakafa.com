## The Outer Electrons that React Most Often

Start with calcium. In the simple shell model, a neutral calcium atom is written with the electron configuration <InlineMath math="2, 8, 8, 2" />. That means calcium's electrons are spread across the <InlineMath math="\mathrm{K}" />, <InlineMath math="\mathrm{L}" />, <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" />, and <InlineMath math="\mathrm{N}" /> shells.

The outermost occupied shell is the <InlineMath math="\mathrm{N}" /> shell. It contains <InlineMath math="2" /> electrons. So, a calcium atom has <InlineMath math="2" /> **valence electrons**.

Valence electrons are electrons in the outermost occupied shell of an atom. OpenStax explains that outer-shell electrons are called valence electrons and play the most important role in chemical reactions. You can read the reference in [Electronic Structure of Atoms from OpenStax](https://openstax.org/books/chemistry/pages/6-4-electronic-structure-of-atoms-electron-configurations).

A simple analogy is an item in the outer pocket of a bag. It is easier to reach, exchange, or use than something buried deep inside. Valence electrons are similar: they do not just fly away on their own, but the outer electrons most strongly shape how an atom bonds, loses electrons, or gains electrons.

## A Fast Way to Identify Valence Electrons

Use the same sequence every time so the idea stays clear.

- Write the shell configuration for the neutral atom.
- Find the last shell that still contains electrons.
- The number of electrons in that last shell is the number of valence electrons.

Examples:

| Atom | Shell configuration | Outermost shell | Valence electrons |
| :--- | :------------------ | :-------------- | :---------------- |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Mg}" /> | <InlineMath math="2, 8, 2" /> | <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> | <InlineMath math="2" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Cl}" /> | <InlineMath math="2, 8, 7" /> | <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> | <InlineMath math="7" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Ar}" /> | <InlineMath math="2, 8, 8" /> | <InlineMath math="\mathrm{M}" /> | <InlineMath math="8" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Ca}" /> | <InlineMath math="2, 8, 8, 2" /> | <InlineMath math="\mathrm{N}" /> | <InlineMath math="2" /> |

Do not add all electrons when looking for valence electrons. Calcium has <InlineMath math="20" /> total electrons, but only <InlineMath math="2" /> valence electrons because only the outermost occupied shell is counted.

## Patterns in the Same Group

Main-group elements in the same group often have the same number of valence electrons. That is why their chemical properties can be similar. OpenStax explains that elements in one group have the same number of valence electrons, and this shared number explains their similar chemical properties. You can read the reference in [Periodic Variations in Element Properties from OpenStax](https://openstax.org/books/chemistry-2e/pages/6-5-periodic-variations-in-element-properties).

Notice the pattern below.

| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Na}" /> | <InlineMath math="11" /> | <InlineMath math="2" /> | <InlineMath math="8" /> | <InlineMath math="1" /> | - | - | - | - | <InlineMath math="1" /> |

| <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Rb}" /> | <InlineMath math="37" /> | <InlineMath math="2" /> | <InlineMath math="8" /> | <InlineMath math="18" /> | <InlineMath math="8" /> | <InlineMath math="1" /> | - | - | <InlineMath math="1" /> |

The table shows the key idea: even though the number of occupied shells increases down the group, the outermost shell still contains <InlineMath math="1" /> electron. This is why lithium, sodium, potassium, rubidium, cesium, and francium are placed in the same group.

Small caution: hydrogen also has <InlineMath math="1" /> valence electron, but it does not behave exactly like the alkali metals. The number of valence electrons is a strong clue, not the only explanation of an element's properties.

## Connection to Chemical Properties

Valence electrons matter because chemical reactions often involve the outer electrons. Atoms can lose, gain, or share valence electrons.

In short:

- In many main-group metals, atoms with <InlineMath math="1" /> or <InlineMath math="2" /> valence electrons tend to lose electrons when forming ions.
- Elements with <InlineMath math="7" /> valence electrons often gain or share <InlineMath math="1" /> electron.
- Noble gases such as helium and argon have full outer shells, so they are very unreactive.

For main-group elements, the quick pattern is:

- Group <InlineMath math="1" /> has <InlineMath math="1" /> valence electron.
- Group <InlineMath math="2" /> has <InlineMath math="2" /> valence electrons.
- Groups <InlineMath math="13" /> to <InlineMath math="18" /> have <InlineMath math="3" /> to <InlineMath math="8" /> valence electrons.

Helium is an important exception: helium is in group <InlineMath math="18" />, but it has only <InlineMath math="2" /> valence electrons because its first shell is full with <InlineMath math="2" /> electrons.

For transition elements, counting valence electrons can be more complicated because <InlineMath math="d" /> subshells can matter. This discussion focuses on main-group elements and the simple shell model.

### Patterns to Notice

Elements <InlineMath math="\mathrm{B}" />, <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Al}" />, <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Ga}" />, <InlineMath math="\mathrm{In}" />, and <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Tl}" /> are in group <InlineMath math="13" />. The quick main-group pattern is <InlineMath math="3" /> valence electrons.

That is why <InlineMath math="\mathrm{Na}" /> and <InlineMath math="\mathrm{K}" /> both tend to form <InlineMath math="+1" /> ions: each has <InlineMath math="1" /> valence electron that is easily lost. Argon is much less reactive than chlorine because argon's outer shell is already full, while chlorine still needs <InlineMath math="1" /> more electron to reach a full-shell pattern.
