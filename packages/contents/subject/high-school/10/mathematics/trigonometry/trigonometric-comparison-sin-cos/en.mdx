## What is the Sine Ratio?

## Trigonometric Ratios in Pyramids

Let's look at an example of applying sine and cosine in the context of pyramids:

### Using Sine to Calculate Pyramid Height

Suppose an archaeologist wants to know the height of a pyramid. They know that the elevation angle from the base to the top of the pyramid is <InlineMath math="41^\circ" /> and the slant height (edge) of the pyramid is <InlineMath math="600 \text{ m}" />.

<Triangle
  title="Calculating Pyramid Height with Sine"
  description="Triangle formed when calculating the height of a pyramid."
  angle={41}
  labels={{
    opposite: "Pyramid Height",
    adjacent: "Base Radius",
    hypotenuse: "Slant Height",
  }}
/>

To calculate the height of the pyramid, we use the sine ratio:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\sin 41^\circ = \frac{\text{pyramid height}}{\text{slant height}}" />
  <BlockMath math="\sin 41^\circ = \frac{x \text{ m}}{600 \text{ m}}" />
  <BlockMath math="0.66 = \frac{x \text{ m}}{600 \text{ m}}" />
  <BlockMath math="x = 0.66 \times 600 \text{ m} = 396 \text{ m}" />
</MathContainer>

Therefore, the height of the pyramid is <InlineMath math="396 \text{ m}" />.

### Using Cosine to Calculate Pyramid Base Radius

Now, if we want to know the base radius of the pyramid, we can use the cosine ratio:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\cos 41^\circ = \frac{\text{pyramid base radius}}{\text{slant height}}" />
  <BlockMath math="\cos 41^\circ = \frac{x \text{ m}}{600 \text{ m}}" />
  <BlockMath math="0.75 = \frac{x \text{ m}}{600 \text{ m}}" />
  <BlockMath math="x = 0.75 \times 600 \text{ m} = 450 \text{ m}" />
</MathContainer>

Therefore, the base radius of the pyramid is <InlineMath math="450 \text{ m}" />.

## Differences and Similarities Between Sin, Cos, and Tan

### Differences

1. **Sine** <InlineMath math="\sin \theta" /> compares the opposite side with the hypotenuse.
2. **Cosine** <InlineMath math="\cos \theta" /> compares the adjacent side with the hypotenuse.
3. **Tangent** <InlineMath math="\tan \theta" /> compares the opposite side with the adjacent side.

### Similarities

1. All three are trigonometric ratios in right triangles.
2. All three change their values according to the angle.
3. These three ratios have a mathematical relationship:

   <BlockMath math="\tan \theta = \frac{\sin \theta}{\cos \theta}" />

## Practice Problem

A child is flying a kite and has managed to raise it to a height of <InlineMath math="3.5 \text{ m}" />. The child is holding the string at a height of <InlineMath math="60 \text{ cm}" /> from the ground. If the kite string forms an angle of <InlineMath math="25^\circ" /> with the ground, what is the length of the string being used?

<Triangle
  title="Kite Problem"
  description="Visualization of the kite string length problem."
  angle={25}
  labels={{
    opposite: "Kite Height",
    adjacent: "Horizontal Distance",
    hypotenuse: "String Length",
  }}
/>

To solve this problem, which trigonometric ratio should we use?

**Correct Solution:**

1. We need to calculate the string length (hypotenuse)
2. We know the effective height of the kite, which is <InlineMath math="3.5 \text{ m} - 0.6 \text{ m} = 2.9 \text{ m}" />.
3. We know the elevation angle <InlineMath math="25^\circ" />.
4. Since we're looking for the hypotenuse and we know the opposite side (effective height), we use the sine ratio:

   <MathContainer>
     <BlockMath math="\sin 25^\circ = \frac{\text{effective height}}{\text{string length}}" />
     <BlockMath math="\sin 25^\circ = \frac{2.9 \text{ m}}{x \text{ m}}" />
     <BlockMath math="0.42 = \frac{2.9 \text{ m}}{x \text{ m}}" />
     <BlockMath math="x = \frac{2.9 \text{ m}}{0.42} = 6.9 \text{ m}" />
   </MathContainer>

Therefore, the length of the kite string being used is approximately <InlineMath math="6.9 \text{ m}" />.
