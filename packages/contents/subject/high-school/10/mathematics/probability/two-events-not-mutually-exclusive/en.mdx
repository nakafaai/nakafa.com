## What Does Non-Mutually Exclusive Mean?

We learned about [mutually exclusive events](/subject/high-school/10/mathematics/probability/two-events-mutually-exclusive) that can't happen together (like turning left and right at the same time). Now, let's talk about **Non-Mutually Exclusive Events**. These are two (or more) events that **CAN happen at the same time** in a single experiment.

This means it's possible to get an outcome that belongs to event <InlineMath math="A" /> and also belongs to event <InlineMath math="B" />.

**Simple Examples:**

1.  **Drawing a Card:** You draw one card from a standard deck.

    - Event <InlineMath math="A" />: Getting a **Heart** (<InlineMath math="\heartsuit" />).
    - Event <InlineMath math="B" />: Getting a **King**.
      Can events <InlineMath math="A" /> and <InlineMath math="B" /> happen together? Absolutely! There's a card that is both a Heart and a King: the **King of Hearts** (<InlineMath math="K\heartsuit" />). Since they can happen together, events <InlineMath math="A" /> and <InlineMath math="B" /> are **non-mutually exclusive**.

2.  **Rolling a Die (once):**
    - Event <InlineMath math="A" />: Getting an **even** number (<InlineMath math="\{2, 4, 6\}" />).
    - Event <InlineMath math="B" />: Getting a number **greater than 3** (<InlineMath math="\{4, 5, 6\}" />).
      Can these happen together? Yes! The numbers <InlineMath math="4" /> and <InlineMath math="6" /> are both even and greater than 3. So, events <InlineMath math="A" /> and <InlineMath math="B" /> are **non-mutually exclusive**.

## The Intersection is Important!

In non-mutually exclusive events, there's a part that belongs to both events simultaneously. This part is called the **intersection**.

Because there is an intersection, the probability of event <InlineMath math="A" /> **AND** B happening together is **greater than zero**.

<BlockMath math="P(A \text{ and} B) > 0" />

Or using the intersection symbol:

<BlockMath math="P(A \cap B) > 0" />

This is very different from mutually exclusive events, where <InlineMath math="P(A \cap B) = 0" />.

## Calculating Combined Probability for Non-Mutually Exclusive Events

Since there's a chance that events <InlineMath math="A" /> and <InlineMath math="B" /> can happen together, we can't just add <InlineMath math="P(A) + P(B)" /> to find <InlineMath math="P(A \text{ or} B)" />.

Why not? Because if we simply add them, the intersection part (<InlineMath math="A \cap B" />) gets **counted twice**, once in <InlineMath math="P(A)" /> and again in <InlineMath math="P(B)" />.

To get the correct calculation, we must **subtract** the probability of the intersection that was double-counted. This gives us the **General Addition Rule** for probability:

<BlockMath math="P(A \text{ or} B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)" />

Or using the union and intersection symbols:

<BlockMath math="P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)" />

This formula works generally, for both mutually exclusive and non-mutually exclusive events. (If they are mutually exclusive, <InlineMath math="P(A \cap B)" /> is zero, so the formula simplifies back to <InlineMath math="P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B)" />).

## Calculation Example

Let's use the card example:

- Event <InlineMath math="A" />: Getting a Heart (<InlineMath math="\heartsuit" />). There are <InlineMath math="13" /> Hearts in <InlineMath math="52 \text{ cards}" />. <InlineMath math="P(A) = 13/52" />.
- Event <InlineMath math="B" />: Getting a King. There are <InlineMath math="4" /> Kings in <InlineMath math="52 \text{ cards}" />. <InlineMath math="P(B) = 4/52" />.
- Event <InlineMath math="A" /> **and** B: Getting the King of Hearts (<InlineMath math="K\heartsuit" />). There is only <InlineMath math="1" /> King of Hearts. <InlineMath math="P(A \cap B) = 1/52" />.

So, the probability of getting a Heart OR a King is:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)" />
  <BlockMath math="= \frac{13}{52} + \frac{4}{52} - \frac{1}{52}" />
  <BlockMath math="= \frac{16}{52} = \frac{4}{13}" />
</MathContainer>

See? We subtract <InlineMath math="1/52" /> so the King of Hearts isn't counted twice.
