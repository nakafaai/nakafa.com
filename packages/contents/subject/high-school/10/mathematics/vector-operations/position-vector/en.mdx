## Definition of Position Vector

A position vector is a vector that starts from point <InlineMath math="O" /> (origin) in a coordinate system and ends at another point. This vector plays an important role in determining the position or location of a point in a coordinate system.

### Characteristics of Position Vectors

Each position vector has the following characteristics:

- Always starts from the origin <InlineMath math="O" /> (center of coordinates)
- Ends at a specific point in the coordinate system
- The coordinates of the position vector are the same as the coordinates of its endpoint

## Representation of Position Vectors

In general, if we have a point <InlineMath math="P" /> with coordinates <InlineMath math="(x, y)" /> in a plane, then the position vector from point <InlineMath math="O" /> to point <InlineMath math="P" /> can be written as <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{OP} = (x, y)" />.

In three-dimensional space, if point <InlineMath math="P" /> has coordinates <InlineMath math="(x, y, z)" />, then its position vector is <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{OP} = (x, y, z)" />.

In the visualization below, we use the notation <InlineMath math="OA" />, <InlineMath math="OB" />, <InlineMath math="OC" />, and <InlineMath math="OD" /> to indicate position vectors from point <InlineMath math="O" /> to specific points (<InlineMath math="A" />, <InlineMath math="B" />, <InlineMath math="C" />, or <InlineMath math="D" />).

## Examples of Position Vectors

Suppose there are two points <InlineMath math="A" /> and <InlineMath math="B" /> in the coordinate plane:

- Point <InlineMath math="A" /> with coordinates <InlineMath math="(-3, 2)" />
- Point <InlineMath math="B" /> with coordinates <InlineMath math="(7, 5)" />

Then the position vectors of these two points are:

- <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{OA} = (-3, 2)" />
- <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{OB} = (7, 5)" />

## Benefits of Position Vectors

Position vectors have several benefits in mathematics and its applications:

1. Determining the location of a point in a coordinate system
2. Serving as a basis for calculating other vectors such as displacement vectors
3. Facilitating the solution of problems related to position and location
4. Used in GPS technology to determine the position of a location

## Relationship with Displacement Vectors

Displacement vectors can be obtained from the difference between two position vectors. If we have position vectors <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{OA}" /> and <InlineMath math="\overrightarrow{OB}" />, then the displacement vector from <InlineMath math="A" /> to <InlineMath math="B" /> is:

<BlockMath math="\overrightarrow{AB} = \overrightarrow{OB} - \overrightarrow{OA}" />

From the previous example, the displacement vector from <InlineMath math="A" /> to <InlineMath math="B" /> is:

<BlockMath
  math="\begin{align}
\overrightarrow{AB} &= \overrightarrow{OB} - \overrightarrow{OA} \\
&= (7, 5) - (-3, 2) \\
&= (7-(-3), 5-2) \\
&= (10, 3)
\end{align}"
/>

Therefore, to move from point <InlineMath math="A" /> to point <InlineMath math="B" />, we need to move <InlineMath math="10 \text{ units}" /> to the right and <InlineMath math="3 \text{ units}" /> upward.
