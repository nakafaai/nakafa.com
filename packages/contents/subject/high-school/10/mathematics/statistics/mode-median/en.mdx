## Measures of Central Tendency

In statistics, we often want to know where the "center" of our data lies. Mode and Median are two ways to see this center or tendency of the data.

## Median: The Middle Value

The median is simply the **data value that is exactly in the middle** after all data has been sorted from smallest to largest.

**How to Find the Median:**

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\text{1. Sort Data: From smallest to largest}" />
  <BlockMath math="\text{2. Find the Middle Position}" />
</MathContainer>

- **If the number of data points is odd (<InlineMath math="n" /> is odd):**

  <BlockMath math="\text{Median} = \text{Value at position } \frac{n+1}{2}" />

  **Example (Odd Data):**

  Suppose the test scores of <InlineMath math="5 \text{ students}" /> are: <InlineMath math="7, 8, 6, 9, 7" />

  Sort: <InlineMath math="6, 7, 7, 8, 9" /> (there are <InlineMath math="5" /> data points, <InlineMath math="n=5" />)

  Middle position: <InlineMath math="(5+1)/2 = 3" />. The data at the <InlineMath math="3^{\text{rd}}" /> position is <InlineMath math="7" />.

  So, <InlineMath math="\text{Median} = 7" />.

- **If the number of data points is even (<InlineMath math="n" /> is even):**

  <BlockMath math="\text{Median} = \frac{\text{Value at position }(n/2) + \text{Value at position }((n/2)+1)}{2}" />

  **Example (Even Data):**

  Suppose the test scores of <InlineMath math="6 \text{ students}" /> are: <InlineMath math="7, 8, 6, 9, 7, 10" />

  Sort: <InlineMath math="6, 7, 7, 8, 9, 10" /> (there are <InlineMath math="6" /> data points, <InlineMath math="n=6" />)

  Middle positions: Position <InlineMath math="6/2 = 3" /> and position <InlineMath math="(6/2) + 1 = 4" />.

  The two middle numbers are <InlineMath math="7" /> and <InlineMath math="8" />.

  Median = Average of 7 and 8 = <InlineMath math="(7+8)/2 = 7.5" />.

  So, Median is <InlineMath math="7.5" />.

The median is useful because it is not heavily affected by extremely large or small values (outliers) at the ends of the data.

## Mode: The Most Frequent Value

The mode is even simpler. The mode is the **data value that appears most often** or has the highest frequency.

**How to Find the Mode:**

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\text{1. Count the frequency of each data value}" />
  <BlockMath math="\text{2. Find the data value with the highest frequency}" />
</MathContainer>

**Example:**

Test scores data:

<BlockMath math="7, 8, 6, 9, 7, 8, 7" />

- Score <InlineMath math="6" /> appears <InlineMath math="1 \text{ time}" />
- Score <InlineMath math="7" /> appears <InlineMath math="3 \text{ times}" />
- Score <InlineMath math="8" /> appears <InlineMath math="2 \text{ times}" />
- Score <InlineMath math="9" /> appears <InlineMath math="1 \text{ time}" />

The most frequent score is <InlineMath math="7" /> (appears <InlineMath math="3 \text{ times}" />).

So, the mode is <InlineMath math="7" />.

**Important Notes about Mode:**

- A dataset might have **no mode** (if all values appear with the same frequency).
- It can have **one mode** (unimodal).
- It can have **two modes** (bimodal), if there are two values with the same highest frequency.
- It can have **more than two modes** (multimodal).

Like the median, the mode is also not affected by extreme values (outliers).

## Range Shows Data Spread

Besides looking at the center of the data with mode and median, sometimes we also need to know how **spread out** our data is. One of the simplest ways is to look at the **Range**.

The range is the **difference between the largest and smallest data values**.

<BlockMath math="\text{Range} = \text{Largest Data} - \text{Smallest Data}" />

**Example:**

Data: 6, 7, 7, 8, 9, 10

Largest data = 10

Smallest data = 6

Range = <InlineMath math="10 - 6 = 4" />

The range gives us a quick idea of how far our data spreads from end to end.

So, Median and Mode focus on the "center" of the data, while Range focuses on the "spread" of the data.
