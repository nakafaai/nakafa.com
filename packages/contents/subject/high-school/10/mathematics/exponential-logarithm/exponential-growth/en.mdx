## Definition of Exponential Growth Function

Exponential growth is a type of growth where **the rate of change is proportional to its quantity**. In this growth, the value increases more rapidly over time.

The exponential growth function is written as:

<InlineMath math="f(x) = a^x" /> where <InlineMath math="a > 1" />

Where:

- <InlineMath math="a" /> is the base (growth constant)
- <InlineMath math="x" /> is the variable (time)

## Examples of Exponential Growth

Exponential growth is often found in real life, such as the growth of bacteria that divide into two at regular time intervals.

### Bacterial Growth

A researcher observes bacterial growth on a host with an initial condition of <InlineMath math="30 \text{ bacteria}" />. These bacteria divide into two every <InlineMath math="30 \text{ minutes}" />.

If <InlineMath math="x" /> is the growth phase of bacteria every <InlineMath math="30 \text{ minutes}" />, then:

| Phase (<InlineMath math="30 \text{ minutes}" />) | <InlineMath math="0" /> | <InlineMath math="1" /> | <InlineMath math="2" /> | <InlineMath math="3" /> | <InlineMath math="4" /> | <InlineMath math="5" /> |
| ------------------ | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Number of bacteria | <InlineMath math="30" /> | <InlineMath math="60" /> | <InlineMath math="120" /> | <InlineMath math="240" /> | <InlineMath math="480" /> | <InlineMath math="960" /> |

It can be observed that:

- For <InlineMath math="x = 0" />, the number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="30" />
- For <InlineMath math="x = 1" />, the number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="60" />
- For <InlineMath math="x = 2" />, the number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="120 = 2^2 \cdot 30" />
- For <InlineMath math="x = 3" />, the number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="240 = 2^3 \cdot 30" />
- For <InlineMath math="x = 4" />, the number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="480 = 2^4 \cdot 30" />

From this pattern, bacterial growth can be modeled with an exponential function:

<BlockMath math="f(x) = 30 \cdot (2^x)" />

### Calculating the Number of Bacteria at a Specific Time

If we want to calculate the number of bacteria at hour <InlineMath math="5" />, we need to know that hour <InlineMath math="5" /> occurs at phase <InlineMath math="10" /> (because each phase is <InlineMath math="30 \text{ minutes}" />):

<BlockMath math="f(10) = 30 \cdot (2^{10}) = 30 \cdot 1024 = 30,720" />

So, at hour <InlineMath math="5" />, there are <InlineMath math="30{,}720 \text{ bacteria}" />.

## Variations in Initial Value

Exponential growth can have different initial values. In general, if the initial value is <InlineMath math="P_0" />, then the exponential growth function becomes:

<BlockMath math="f(x) = P_0 \cdot (a^x)" />

For example:

- If the initial number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="50" />, then <InlineMath math="f(x) = 50 \cdot (2^x)" />
- If the initial number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="100" />, then <InlineMath math="f(x) = 100 \cdot (2^x)" />
- If the initial number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="200" />, then <InlineMath math="f(x) = 200 \cdot (2^x)" />

### Determining the Initial Value

Sometimes we need to determine the initial value <InlineMath math="x_0" /> of an exponential growth if we know its value at a certain time.

**Example case**:

Suppose we know that the number of bacteria at phase <InlineMath math="2" /> is <InlineMath math="8{,}000" /> and the bacteria divide into two at each time interval. What is the initial number of bacteria?

We can use the equation <InlineMath math="x_2 = a^2 \cdot x_0" /> with <InlineMath math="a = 2" />:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="x_2 = a^2 \cdot x_0" />
  <BlockMath math="8000 = 2^2 \cdot x_0" />
  <BlockMath math="8000 = 4 \cdot x_0" />
  <BlockMath math="\frac{8000}{4} = x_0" />
  <BlockMath math="x_0 = 2000" />
</MathContainer>

So, the initial number of bacteria is <InlineMath math="2{,}000 \text{ bacteria}" />.

### Calculating Long-Term Growth

To calculate the number of bacteria after a longer time, we still use the same model. For example, to calculate the number of bacteria after <InlineMath math="10 \text{ hours}" />:

<BlockMath math="x_{10} = a^{10} \cdot x_0" />

Substitute the values <InlineMath math="a = 2" /> and <InlineMath math="x_0 = 2,000" />:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="x_{10} = 2^{10} \cdot 2,000" />
  <BlockMath math="x_{10} = 1,024 \cdot 2,000" />
  <BlockMath math="x_{10} = 2,048,000" />
</MathContainer>

So, the number of bacteria after <InlineMath math="10 \text{ hours}" /> is <InlineMath math="2{,}048{,}000 \text{ bacteria}" />.

## Other Applications of Exponential Growth

Exponential growth is also found in other contexts such as:

- Compound interest in investments
- Population growth of living organisms
- Radioactive decay (negative growth)
- Spread of infectious diseases

Exponential growth is very important in various fields such as biology, finance, and physics because it describes many real-world phenomena.
