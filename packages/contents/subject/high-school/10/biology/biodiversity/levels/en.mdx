## One Market Can Show Three Scales

Picture yourself standing in a fruit market. On one table, several bananas differ in taste, size, and shape. On another table, you see mangoes, oranges, chilies, fungi, fish, and chicken. Even in that small place, biodiversity can be read at several scales.

**Biodiversity** is the variation of living things. This variation can appear at the genetic level, species level, and ecosystem level. A **gene** is a part of genetic material that influences a trait. A **species** is a group of organisms with shared basic traits that generally produce the same kind of offspring. An **ecosystem** is the relationship between organisms and their physical environment.

This layered reading matches the way biology discusses biodiversity from genes to ecosystems. The broader overview is available in [OpenStax Biology 2e](https://openstax.org/books/biology-2e/pages/47-1-the-biodiversity-crisis).

## Genes Explain Variation Within One Kind

Genetic diversity appears when organisms are still one species, but their traits are not exactly the same. For example, banana varieties can differ in fruit size, taste, shape, or resistance to environmental conditions. They remain close as one group, but genetic differences create trait variation.

Think of several recipes for one kind of food. The basic dish is the same, but different amounts of ingredients can change taste and texture. In organisms, those differences are read as genetic variation.

Genetic variation matters because it supplies raw material for adaptation. If the environment changes, a population with more variation has a better chance of including individuals that can cope.

## Species and Ecosystems Need a Wider View

Species diversity no longer asks "why is this banana different from another banana". The question becomes "what kinds of organisms live here". In a garden, we may find trees, insects, birds, fungi, bacteria, and small animals. Each kind has its own traits and role.

Ecosystem diversity is wider again. Tropical rainforests, mangroves, savannas, rice fields, and coral reefs have different conditions. Light, water, salinity, soil, temperature, and organism interactions shape which living things can survive there.

Indonesia is a strong example because it is large, tropical, and rich in habitat types. Western fauna tends to differ from eastern fauna, while the central region contains many transitional forms. This biodiversity is not just a list of organism names. It is the result of environment, history, and long biological interactions.

## Reading the Scale Makes Observation Sharper

When observing nature, do not immediately write everything as "many kinds". Ask for the scale first. If the comparison is still within one kind but traits differ, you are reading genes. If the comparison is between many organism kinds, you are reading species. If the comparison is about living places and environmental relationships, you are reading ecosystems.

This scale makes observation neater. One market, garden, or forest can contain all three levels at once. What changes is the question we ask, not only the object we see.
