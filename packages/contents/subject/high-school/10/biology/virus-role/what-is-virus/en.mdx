## A Tiny Particle That Changes the Question

A virus is very small and can reproduce only after entering a living cell. Because it has no cell structure, cytoplasm, ribosomes, or independent metabolism, a virus is usually studied as a biological particle, not a cellular organism.

OpenStax Biology 2e explains that viruses are acellular and depend on host cells for replication in [Viruses](https://openstax.org/books/biology-2e/pages/21-1-viral-evolution-morphology-and-classification).

## Why a Virus Is Not a Cell

Living cells can run chemical reactions, make proteins, and regulate their internal environment. A virus cannot do that by itself. It carries instructions, but it needs a host cell's machinery to copy and express those instructions.

This distinction matters. If we treat a virus as a tiny cell, we misunderstand how medicines, vaccines, and immune defenses work against it.

## Self-Check

If a particle has a capsid and RNA but no ribosomes of its own, can it make proteins by itself?

No. Without ribosomes, it must use a host cell's ribosomes to make viral proteins.
