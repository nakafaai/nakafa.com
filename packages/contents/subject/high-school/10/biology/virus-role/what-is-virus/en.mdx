## A virus is an acellular particle

A virus is often mistaken for a tiny cell. That is not quite right. A virus is acellular, meaning it is not organized as a complete cell. The Indonesian grade 10 science textbook describes a virion, one complete virus particle, as nucleic acid packaged in a capsid. OpenStax also describes viruses as noncellular entities that lack organelles, ribosomes, and a plasma membrane in [Viral Morphology](https://openstax.org/books/biology-2e/pages/21-1-viral-evolution-morphology-and-classification).

## Virus parts are not cell parts

Cells have cytoplasm, ribosomes, a cell membrane, and metabolic machinery. Viruses do not carry that full system. That is why viruses cannot reproduce by themselves outside a cell. They need a host cell, the cell used to copy genetic material and build new viral parts.

The size also matters. The Indonesian textbook gives a range around 20 to 400 nm, and OpenStax places many virions in the tens to hundreds of nanometers. That scale explains why ordinary light microscopes are not enough to observe most viruses.

## Self-check

If a virus does not have ribosomes, which part of the host cell becomes essential for making viral proteins?

Answer: the host cell's ribosomes.

Explanation: the viral genome carries instructions, but protein production still depends on the host cell's translation machinery.
