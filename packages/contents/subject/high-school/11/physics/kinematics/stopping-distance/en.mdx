## Stopping Distance Is More Than Braking Distance

Stopping distance is the total distance a moving object needs until it fully stops. For a vehicle, this distance does not only happen while the brakes are acting.

Before braking begins, the driver still needs reaction time. During that time, the vehicle keeps moving forward with its initial speed.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="d_{\text{stop}}=d_{\text{reaction}}+d_{\text{brake}}" />
  <BlockMath math="d_{\text{reaction}}=vt_r" />
</MathContainer>

So stopping distance has two stages. The first stage happens when the driver notices danger but the vehicle has not started braking. The second stage happens after the brakes act and the vehicle slows to a stop.

| Part | What Happens | What Determines It |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Reaction distance | The vehicle still moves with its initial speed | Speed and reaction time |
| Braking distance | The vehicle slows until it stops | Initial speed and deceleration magnitude |

## Braking Distance Comes from Uniform Acceleration

While the brakes act, the vehicle slows down. If the deceleration is treated as constant and the final velocity is zero, a uniformly accelerated motion equation can be used to find the braking distance.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="v_t^2=v_0^2+2a\Delta x" />
  <BlockMath math="0=v^2-2b\,d_{\text{brake}}" />
  <BlockMath math="d_{\text{brake}}=\frac{v^2}{2b}" />
</MathContainer>

The quantity <InlineMath math="b" /> is the magnitude of the deceleration, so it is written as a positive value.

This formula uses a simple assumption: the path is straight, braking deceleration is treated as constant, and the vehicle does not skid. If the road condition changes, the value of <InlineMath math="b" /> can change too.

## Speed Makes Stopping Distance Grow Quickly

Reaction distance is directly proportional to speed. If the speed doubles, the reaction distance also doubles for the same reaction time.

Braking distance is more sensitive because it contains <InlineMath math="v^2" />. As speed increases, the motion energy that must be removed by the brakes grows much more quickly.

Suppose the reaction time is <InlineMath math="1\text{ s}" /> and the braking deceleration magnitude is <InlineMath math="5\text{ m/s}^2" />.

| Speed | Reaction Distance | Braking Distance | Stopping Distance |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| <InlineMath math="10\text{ m/s}" /> | <InlineMath math="10\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="10\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="20\text{ m}" /> |
| <InlineMath math="20\text{ m/s}" /> | <InlineMath math="20\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="40\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="60\text{ m}" /> |
| <InlineMath math="30\text{ m/s}" /> | <InlineMath math="30\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="90\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="120\text{ m}" /> |

When speed rises from <InlineMath math="10" /> to <InlineMath math="20\text{ m/s}" />, stopping distance rises from <InlineMath math="20" /> to <InlineMath math="60\text{ m}" />. It is not just doubled because the braking part follows the square of speed.

## Safe Space Before a Vehicle Stops

A vehicle moves at <InlineMath math="20\text{ m/s}" />. The driver's reaction time is <InlineMath math="1\text{ s}" /> and the magnitude of the braking deceleration is <InlineMath math="5\text{ m/s}^2" />.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="d_{\text{reaction}}=20(1)=20\text{ m}" />
  <BlockMath math="d_{\text{brake}}=\frac{20^2}{2(5)}=40\text{ m}" />
  <BlockMath math="d_{\text{stop}}=20+40=60\text{ m}" />
</MathContainer>

So the vehicle needs <InlineMath math="60\text{ m}" /> to stop under those conditions.

That <InlineMath math="60\text{ m}" /> is not only the distance after the brakes are pressed. The first <InlineMath math="20\text{ m}" /> happens before braking starts, and the next <InlineMath math="40\text{ m}" /> happens while the vehicle is braking.
