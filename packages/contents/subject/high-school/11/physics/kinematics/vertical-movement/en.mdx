## A Vertical Axis Needs a Positive Direction

Vertical motion is upward or downward motion along one straight line. To keep the signs clear, we choose upward as positive and downward as negative.

With that choice, gravitational acceleration is written as <InlineMath math="-g" /> because gravity always pulls the object downward.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="y=y_0+v_0t-\frac{1}{2}gt^2" />
  <BlockMath math="v_y=v_0-gt" />
  <BlockMath math="v_y^2=v_0^2-2g(y-y_0)" />
</MathContainer>

## Gravity Still Acts While the Object Rises

When an object is thrown upward, its initial velocity is positive. After that, gravity reduces its vertical velocity little by little until the object stops for an instant at the highest point.

> At the highest point, the vertical velocity is momentarily zero, but gravitational acceleration still points downward.

This idea matters because students often think gravity disappears at the top. Only the velocity is zero for an instant, not the acceleration.

## An Upward Throw Stops for an Instant

Suppose a ball is thrown upward with initial velocity <InlineMath math="20\text{ m/s}" /> from position <InlineMath math="y_0=0" />. Use <InlineMath math="g=10\text{ m/s}^2" />. At the highest point, its vertical velocity is <InlineMath math="v_y=0" />.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="v_y=v_0-gt" />
  <BlockMath math="0=20-10t" />
  <BlockMath math="t=2\text{ s}" />
</MathContainer>

The maximum height above the launch point can be found from the position equation.

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
y-y_0&=v_0t-\frac{1}{2}gt^2\\
&=20(2)-\frac{1}{2}(10)(2^2)\\
&=20\text{ m}
\end{aligned}" />

So the ball takes <InlineMath math="2\text{ s}" /> to reach the top and rises <InlineMath math="20\text{ m}" /> above its starting point.

## Reading the Sign of Velocity

The sign of <InlineMath math="v_y" /> tells the direction of motion at that moment. A positive value means the object is moving upward, a negative value means it is moving downward, and zero means it is momentarily stopped.

Notice that height <InlineMath math="y" /> does not automatically tell the motion direction. An object can be at the same height while moving upward and while moving downward, but the velocity signs are different.

| Value of <InlineMath math="v_y" /> | Motion Direction | Example Situation |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Positive | Upward | A ball just thrown upward |
| Zero | Stopped for an instant | A ball at the highest point |
| Negative | Downward | A ball falling back down |

## A Ball Dropped From a Height

Suppose a ball is dropped from a height of <InlineMath math="20\text{ m}" /> with no initial velocity. With <InlineMath math="g=10\text{ m/s}^2" />, the time to reach the ground is:

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
0&=20-\frac{1}{2}(10)t^2\\
5t^2&=20\\
t&=2\text{ s}
\end{aligned}" />

The velocity just before reaching the ground is:

<BlockMath math="v_y=0-10(2)=-20\text{ m/s}" />

The negative sign shows the downward direction. So the speed is <InlineMath math="20\text{ m/s}" />, but the motion direction is downward.

## From Vertical Motion to Projectile Motion

Vertical motion also appears in projectile motion. Later, when an object moves at an angle, the horizontal part is read as sideways motion, while the vertical part still follows the gravity equations from this lesson.

That is why free-fall motion is useful beyond this page. The vertical part of a thrown ball, a projectile, or water leaving a hose is still shaped by gravitational acceleration.
