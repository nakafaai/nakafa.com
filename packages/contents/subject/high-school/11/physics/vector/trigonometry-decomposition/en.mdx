## The Angle Determines the Components

When a vector forms an angle with an axis, its components can be found using trigonometry. The idea is simple: the slanted vector becomes the hypotenuse of a right triangle, while its components become the horizontal and vertical sides.

If angle <InlineMath math="\theta" /> is measured from the positive <InlineMath math="x" /> axis, the horizontal component is adjacent to the angle, while the vertical component is opposite the angle.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="F_x=F\cos\theta" />
  <BlockMath math="F_y=F\sin\theta" />
</MathContainer>

> Do not memorize where <InlineMath math="\sin" /> and <InlineMath math="\cos" /> go without checking the reference angle.

## A Slanted Force on Coordinate Axes

Imagine a rope pulling a box with force <InlineMath math="\vec{F}" /> up and to the right. The pull has a horizontal part that pulls the box rightward and a vertical part that lifts slightly upward.

The endpoint of <InlineMath math="F_x" /> followed by <InlineMath math="F_y" /> matches the endpoint of vector <InlineMath math="\vec{F}" />. The components are not extra forces. They are another way to describe the same force.

## Worked Calculation

A <InlineMath math="50\text{ N}" /> force acts <InlineMath math="37^\circ" /> above the horizontal. Use <InlineMath math="\cos37^\circ\approx0.8" /> and <InlineMath math="\sin37^\circ\approx0.6" />.

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
F_x&=F\cos\theta \\
&=50(0.8) \\
&=40\text{ N}
\end{aligned}" />

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
F_y&=F\sin\theta \\
&=50(0.6) \\
&=30\text{ N}
\end{aligned}" />

So the slanted <InlineMath math="50\text{ N}" /> force is equivalent to a <InlineMath math="40\text{ N}" /> pull to the right and a <InlineMath math="30\text{ N}" /> pull upward.

## If the Angle Is Measured from the Vertical Axis

The formulas change when the reference angle changes. If angle <InlineMath math="\theta" /> is measured from the <InlineMath math="y" /> axis, the component adjacent to the angle is <InlineMath math="F_y" />.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="F_y=F\cos\theta" />
  <BlockMath math="F_x=F\sin\theta" />
</MathContainer>

Before using a formula, always ask: from which axis is the angle measured? This small check prevents a common error when resolving vectors.

## Component Signs Follow the Quadrant

The values of <InlineMath math="\sin" /> and <InlineMath math="\cos" /> give component sizes, but positive or negative signs come from the vector direction on the coordinate axes.

| Vector direction | Sign of <InlineMath math="F_x" /> | Sign of <InlineMath math="F_y" /> |
| :--------------- | :------------------------------- | :------------------------------- |
| up and right | positive | positive |
| up and left | negative | positive |
| down and left | negative | negative |
| down and right | positive | negative |

If a force points up and to the left, its horizontal component is negative because it opposes the positive <InlineMath math="x" /> axis, while its vertical component remains positive.
