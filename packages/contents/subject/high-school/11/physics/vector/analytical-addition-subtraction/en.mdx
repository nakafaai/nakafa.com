## Calculating a Resultant with Components

The graphical method helps us see the resultant direction, but its accuracy depends on the drawing. The analytical method is more precise because it uses component calculations.

The method is to resolve every vector into the <InlineMath math="x" /> and <InlineMath math="y" /> axes, add the components along each axis, and then rebuild the resultant.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="R_x=\sum F_x" />
  <BlockMath math="R_y=\sum F_y" />
</MathContainer>

> In the analytical method, vectors are added axis by axis, not directly from their arrow lengths.

## Steps for Vector Addition

Suppose several forces <InlineMath math="\vec{F}_1" />, <InlineMath math="\vec{F}_2" />, and <InlineMath math="\vec{F}_3" /> act on an object. Each force is first split into horizontal and vertical components.

After all components are known, the resultant components are:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="R_x=F_{1x}+F_{2x}+F_{3x}+\cdots" />
  <BlockMath math="R_y=F_{1y}+F_{2y}+F_{3y}+\cdots" />
</MathContainer>

The resultant magnitude is:

<BlockMath math="R=\sqrt{R_x^2+R_y^2}" />

The direction of the resultant relative to the positive <InlineMath math="x" /> axis can be found from:

<BlockMath math="\tan\theta=\frac{R_y}{R_x}" />

If <InlineMath math="R_x" /> or <InlineMath math="R_y" /> is negative, identify the quadrant first so the direction is not misplaced.

## Example: Two-Stage Displacement

A person walks in two stages. The first stage is equivalent to <InlineMath math="80\text{ m}" /> east and <InlineMath math="60\text{ m}" /> north. The second stage is equivalent to <InlineMath math="120\text{ m}" /> east and <InlineMath math="90\text{ m}" /> north.

| Displacement vector | <InlineMath math="x" /> component | <InlineMath math="y" /> component |
| :------------------ | :-------------------------------- | :-------------------------------- |
| <InlineMath math="\vec{s}_1" /> | <InlineMath math="80\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="60\text{ m}" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\vec{s}_2" /> | <InlineMath math="120\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="90\text{ m}" /> |
| <InlineMath math="\vec{R}" /> | <InlineMath math="200\text{ m}" /> | <InlineMath math="150\text{ m}" /> |

The resultant components are:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="R_x=80+120=200\text{ m}" />
  <BlockMath math="R_y=60+90=150\text{ m}" />
</MathContainer>

The total displacement magnitude is:

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
R&=\sqrt{200^2+150^2} \\
&=\sqrt{40000+22500} \\
&=250\text{ m}
\end{aligned}" />

The resultant direction is:

<BlockMath math="\tan\theta=\frac{150}{200}=0.75" />

Therefore <InlineMath math="\theta\approx36.9^\circ" /> above east. The total displacement is <InlineMath math="250\text{ m}" /> at <InlineMath math="36.9^\circ" /> north of east.

## Subtraction with Components

Vector subtraction is also done component by component. If <InlineMath math="\vec{C}=\vec{A}-\vec{B}" />, then:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="C_x=A_x-B_x" />
  <BlockMath math="C_y=A_y-B_y" />
</MathContainer>

For example, let <InlineMath math="\vec{A}=7\hat{i}+4\hat{j}" /> and <InlineMath math="\vec{B}=2\hat{i}+6\hat{j}" />.

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
\vec{C}&=(7-2)\hat{i}+(4-6)\hat{j} \\
&=5\hat{i}-2\hat{j}
\end{aligned}" />

The negative sign in <InlineMath math="-2\hat{j}" /> means the resulting <InlineMath math="y" /> component points downward.
