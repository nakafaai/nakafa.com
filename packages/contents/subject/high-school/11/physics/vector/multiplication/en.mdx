## Two Kinds of Vector Multiplication

Vector multiplication does not always produce another vector. Two kinds of multiplication are common in physics: the **dot product** and the **cross product**.

The dot product produces a scalar. The cross product produces a new vector whose direction is perpendicular to the two original vectors.

| Type of multiplication | Symbol | Result | Physics example |
| :--------------------- | :----- | :----- | :-------------- |
| dot product | <InlineMath math="\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B}" /> | scalar | work |
| cross product | <InlineMath math="\vec{A}\times\vec{B}" /> | vector | torque |

> The multiplication sign changes because the physical question changes: how much is in the same direction, or how strong is the turning effect.

## Dot Product Measures the Same-Direction Part

The dot product of <InlineMath math="\vec{A}" /> and <InlineMath math="\vec{B}" /> is defined as:

<BlockMath math="\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B}=AB\cos\theta" />

Angle <InlineMath math="\theta" /> is the angle between the two vectors. The result is a scalar because it gives a value, not a direction.

In physics, work done by a force can be written as:

<BlockMath math="W=\vec{F}\cdot\vec{s}=Fs\cos\theta" />

This means only the component of force along the displacement actually does work.

## Example: Work with Dot Product

A person pulls a box with a <InlineMath math="40\text{ N}" /> force while the box moves <InlineMath math="5\text{ m}" />. The force makes a <InlineMath math="60^\circ" /> angle with the displacement direction. The work is:

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
W&=Fs\cos\theta \\
&=40(5)\cos60^\circ \\
&=200(0.5) \\
&=100\text{ J}
\end{aligned}" />

If the force is perpendicular to the displacement, <InlineMath math="\theta=90^\circ" /> and <InlineMath math="\cos90^\circ=0" />. In that case, the force does no work in the displacement direction.

## Cross Product Measures Turning Effect

The cross product of <InlineMath math="\vec{A}" /> and <InlineMath math="\vec{B}" /> has magnitude:

<BlockMath math="\lvert\vec{A}\times\vec{B}\rvert=AB\sin\theta" />

The result is a vector perpendicular to the plane containing <InlineMath math="\vec{A}" /> and <InlineMath math="\vec{B}" />. Its direction is determined by the right-hand rule.

For torque, the cross product is written:

<BlockMath math="\vec{\tau}=\vec{r}\times\vec{F}" />

Vector <InlineMath math="\vec{r}" /> is the lever arm from the rotation axis to the point where the force acts. The same force produces a larger torque when applied farther from a door hinge.

## Example: Torque

A door is pushed with a <InlineMath math="30\text{ N}" /> force at a distance of <InlineMath math="0.8\text{ m}" /> from the hinge. The force is perpendicular to the door, so <InlineMath math="\theta=90^\circ" />.

<BlockMath math="\begin{aligned}
\tau&=rF\sin\theta \\
&=0.8(30)\sin90^\circ \\
&=24\text{ N m}
\end{aligned}" />

If the force is applied at a smaller angle, <InlineMath math="\sin\theta" /> is also smaller. That is why pushing a door perpendicularly is usually more effective than pushing almost parallel to the door surface.

## Comparing Dot and Cross Products

The main difference can be seen from the angle. The dot product is largest when two vectors point the same way because <InlineMath math="\cos0^\circ=1" />. The cross product is zero in that same condition because <InlineMath math="\sin0^\circ=0" />.

| Angle condition | <InlineMath math="\vec{A}\cdot\vec{B}" /> | <InlineMath math="\lvert\vec{A}\times\vec{B}\rvert" /> |
| :-------------- | :---------------------------------------- | :------------------------------------------------------ |
| <InlineMath math="0^\circ" /> | maximum | zero |
| <InlineMath math="90^\circ" /> | zero | maximum |
| <InlineMath math="180^\circ" /> | negative | zero |

So the dot product is useful for asking about alignment, while the cross product is useful for perpendicular effects such as rotation.
