Given <InlineMath math="\angle BOC = 50^\circ" />.
Line <InlineMath math="AOB" /> is a straight line (diameter), so the straight angle is <InlineMath math="180^\circ" />.

Thus, we can find the measure of <InlineMath math="\angle AOC" />:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\angle AOC + \angle BOC = 180^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="\angle AOC + 50^\circ = 180^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="\angle AOC = 180^\circ - 50^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="\angle AOC = 130^\circ" />
</MathContainer>

Consider triangle <InlineMath math="\triangle AOC" />.
Since <InlineMath math="O" /> is the center of the circle, <InlineMath math="OA" /> and <InlineMath math="OC" /> are radii of the circle.
Therefore, <InlineMath math="OA = OC" />, so triangle <InlineMath math="\triangle AOC" /> is an isosceles triangle.

In an isosceles triangle <InlineMath math="\triangle AOC" />, the base angles are equal, i.e., <InlineMath math="\angle OAC = \angle OCA" />.

The sum of angles in a triangle is <InlineMath math="180^\circ" />.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\angle OAC + \angle OCA + \angle AOC = 180^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="2(\angle OAC) + 130^\circ = 180^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="2(\angle OAC) = 180^\circ - 130^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="2(\angle OAC) = 50^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="\angle OAC = \frac{50^\circ}{2}" />
  <BlockMath math="\angle OAC = 25^\circ" />
</MathContainer>

So, the measure of <InlineMath math="\angle OAC" /> is <InlineMath math="25^\circ" />.
