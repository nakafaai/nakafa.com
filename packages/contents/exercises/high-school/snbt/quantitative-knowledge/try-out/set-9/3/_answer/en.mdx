We draw an auxiliary line parallel to lines <InlineMath math="PQ" /> and <InlineMath math="RS" /> passing through the vertex of angle <InlineMath math="\alpha" />.
This line divides angle <InlineMath math="\alpha" /> into two parts, <InlineMath math="\beta" /> and <InlineMath math="\gamma" />.

#### Finding the Value of <InlineMath math="\beta" />

Notice that angle <InlineMath math="\beta" /> and the <InlineMath math="25^\circ" /> angle are alternate interior angles.
Since the lines are parallel, their measures are equal:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\beta = 25^\circ" />
</MathContainer>

#### Finding the Value of <InlineMath math="\gamma" />

Angle <InlineMath math="\gamma" /> and the <InlineMath math="135^\circ" /> angle are consecutive interior angles.
The sum of these two angles is <InlineMath math="180^\circ" />:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\gamma + 135^\circ = 180^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="\gamma = 180^\circ - 135^\circ = 45^\circ" />
</MathContainer>

#### Calculating the Value of <InlineMath math="\alpha" />

The value of <InlineMath math="\alpha" /> is the sum of <InlineMath math="\beta" /> and <InlineMath math="\gamma" />:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\alpha = \beta + \gamma" />
  <BlockMath math="\alpha = 25^\circ + 45^\circ" />
  <BlockMath math="\alpha = 70^\circ" />
</MathContainer>

Therefore, the value of <InlineMath math="\alpha" /> is <InlineMath math="70^\circ" />.
