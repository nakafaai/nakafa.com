Let:
-   <InlineMath math="P(A)" /> = Probability Ali passes
-   <InlineMath math="P(B)" /> = Probability Bima passes
-   <InlineMath math="P(D)" /> = Probability Dika passes

Given:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="P(A) = 0.75 \rightarrow P(A^c) = 1 - 0.75 = 0.25" />
  <BlockMath math="P(B) = 0.8 \rightarrow P(B^c) = 1 - 0.8 = 0.2" />
  <BlockMath math="P(D) = 0.85 \rightarrow P(D^c) = 1 - 0.85 = 0.15" />
</MathContainer>

If only one person passes, there are three mutually exclusive possibilities:

#### Ali passes, Bima and Dika fail

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="P(A) \cdot P(B^c) \cdot P(D^c) = 0.75 \cdot 0.2 \cdot 0.15" />
  <BlockMath math="= 0.0225" />
</MathContainer>

#### 2. Bima passes, Ali and Dika fail

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="P(B) \cdot P(A^c) \cdot P(D^c) = 0.8 \cdot 0.25 \cdot 0.15" />
  <BlockMath math="= 0.03" />
</MathContainer>

#### Dika passes, Ali and Bima fail

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="P(D) \cdot P(A^c) \cdot P(B^c) = 0.85 \cdot 0.25 \cdot 0.2" />
  <BlockMath math="= 0.0425" />
</MathContainer>

#### Total Probability

Thus, the probability that only one person passes is the sum of these three possibilities:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="0.0225 + 0.03 + 0.0425 = 0.095" />
</MathContainer>
