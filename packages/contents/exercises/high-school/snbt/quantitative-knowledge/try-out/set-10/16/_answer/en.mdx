Let <InlineMath math="x" /> be the number of students who like all three subjects (Mathematics, Physics, and Biology).

We can use the inclusion-exclusion principle for sets:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\begin{aligned} n(M \cup B \cup P) &= n(M) + n(B) + n(P) \\ &- (n(M \cap B) + n(M \cap P) + n(B \cap P)) \\ &+ n(M \cap B \cap P) \end{aligned}" />
</MathContainer>

Given:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="n(M \cup B \cup P) = 50" />
  <BlockMath math="n(M) = 35" />
  <BlockMath math="n(B) = 20" />
  <BlockMath math="n(P) = 15" />
  <BlockMath math="n(M \cap B) = 13" />
  <BlockMath math="n(M \cap P) = 7" />
  <BlockMath math="n(B \cap P) = 5" />
  <BlockMath math="n(M \cap B \cap P) = x" />
</MathContainer>

Substitute these values into the formula:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="50 = 35 + 20 + 15 - (13 + 7 + 5) + x" />
  <BlockMath math="50 = 70 - 25 + x" />
  <BlockMath math="50 = 45 + x" />
  <BlockMath math="x = 5" />
</MathContainer>

Therefore, the number of students who like all three subjects is <InlineMath math="5" />.
