We will calculate the normal income first, then determine the new price and new sales quantity so that the income remains the same but sales increase.

#### Calculating Normal Income

Normal income (<InlineMath math="I_{\text{normal}}" />) is:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="I_{\text{normal}} = \text{Price} \times \text{Quantity Sold}" />
  <BlockMath math="I_{\text{normal}} = 12,000 \times 200" />
  <BlockMath math="I_{\text{normal}} = 2,400,000" />
</MathContainer>

#### Determining Relationship between Price and New Sales

Let <InlineMath math="x" /> be the number of price reductions of <InlineMath math="\text{Rp}1,000.00" />.
- New Price: <InlineMath math="H = 12,000 - 1,000x" />
- New Quantity Sold: <InlineMath math="J = 200 + 20x" />

We want the income to remain the same (<InlineMath math="\text{Rp}2,400,000.00" />):

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="H \times J = 2,400,000" />
  <BlockMath math="(12,000 - 1,000x)(200 + 20x) = 2,400,000" />
</MathContainer>

We simplify by dividing the factors (e.g., divide by <InlineMath math="1,000" /> on price and by <InlineMath math="20" /> on quantity):

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="(12 - x)(10 + x) = \frac{2,400,000}{1,000 \times 20}" />
  <BlockMath math="(12 - x)(10 + x) = 120" />
  <BlockMath math="120 + 12x - 10x - x^2 = 120" />
  <BlockMath math="2x - x^2 = 0" />
  <BlockMath math="x(2 - x) = 0" />
</MathContainer>

We obtain two solutions:
1. <InlineMath math="x = 0" /> (Normal condition, no change)
2. <InlineMath math="x = 2" /> (Price reduction occurs)

Since the shop owner wants to **increase** sales, we choose <InlineMath math="x = 2" />.

#### Calculating Number of Iced Coffees Sold

Substitute <InlineMath math="x = 2" /> into the new quantity formula:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="J = 200 + 20(2)" />
  <BlockMath math="J = 200 + 40" />
  <BlockMath math="J = 240" />
</MathContainer>

So, the number of iced coffees sold is 240 cups.
