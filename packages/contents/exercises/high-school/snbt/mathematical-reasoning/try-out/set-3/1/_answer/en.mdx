**Delivery Period Analysis**

April <InlineMath math="2023" /> has a total of <InlineMath math="30" /> days. The first delivery was made on April <InlineMath math="1" />, which corresponds to day <InlineMath math="1" />. We need to calculate how many times suppliers A and B delivered goods within that <InlineMath math="30" />-day period.

The delivery schedule follows an Arithmetic Progression pattern with the first term <InlineMath math="a = 1" /> (first delivery date) and common difference <InlineMath math="b" /> corresponding to each supplier's interval. The condition for the delivery date is <InlineMath math="U_n \le 30" />.

**Cooking Oil A Volume**

Supplier A delivers every <InlineMath math="3" /> days.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="1 + (n_A - 1)3 \le 30" />
  <BlockMath math="(n_A - 1)3 \le 29" />
  <BlockMath math="n_A - 1 \le 9.66..." />
  <BlockMath math="n_A \le 10.66..." />
</MathContainer>

Thus, supplier A delivers <InlineMath math="10" /> times.

Total volume A:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="10 \times 10 = 100 \text{ liters}" />
</MathContainer>

**Cooking Oil B Volume**

Supplier B delivers every <InlineMath math="4" /> days.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="1 + (n_B - 1)4 \le 30" />
  <BlockMath math="(n_B - 1)4 \le 29" />
  <BlockMath math="n_B - 1 \le 7.25" />
  <BlockMath math="n_B \le 8.25" />
</MathContainer>

Thus, supplier B delivers <InlineMath math="8" /> times.

Total volume B:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="8 \times 15 = 120 \text{ liters}" />
</MathContainer>

**Combined Total Volume**

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\text{Total} = \text{Volume A} + \text{Volume B}" />
  <BlockMath math="\text{Total} = 100 + 120 = 220 \text{ liters}" />
</MathContainer>

Therefore, the total volume of cooking oil brands A and B received is <InlineMath math="220" /> liters.
