The relationship between the speeds of vehicles <InlineMath math="X" />, <InlineMath math="Y" />, and <InlineMath math="Z" /> is given by:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="V_X = 2V_Y" />
  <BlockMath math="V_Z = 2V_X = 2(2V_Y) = 4V_Y \implies V_Y = \frac{1}{4}V_Z" />
</MathContainer>

Next, we calculate the speed of vehicle <InlineMath math="Z" /> (<InlineMath math="V_Z" />). It is known that <InlineMath math="Z" /> travels a distance of <InlineMath math="40" /> km in <InlineMath math="2" /> hours.

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="V_Z = \frac{\text{Distance}}{\text{Time}} = \frac{40 \text{ km}}{2 \text{ hours}} = 20 \text{ km/h}" />
</MathContainer>

After finding <InlineMath math="V_Z" />, we can determine the speed of vehicle <InlineMath math="Y" /> (<InlineMath math="V_Y" />):

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="V_Y = \frac{1}{4}V_Z = \frac{1}{4}(20) = 5 \text{ km/h}" />
</MathContainer>

Thus, the distance vehicle <InlineMath math="Y" /> can travel in <InlineMath math="3" /> hours is:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\text{Distance}_Y = V_Y \times \text{Time} = 5 \times 3 = 15 \text{ km}" />
</MathContainer>

So, vehicle <InlineMath math="Y" /> can travel a distance of <InlineMath math="15" /> km.
