We can simplify the statements into mathematical logic forms.

#### Variable Definitions

Let:
*   <InlineMath math="p" />: <InlineMath math="A = B" />
*   <InlineMath math="q" />: <InlineMath math="C \neq D" />
*   <InlineMath math="r" />: <InlineMath math="E = F" />
*   <InlineMath math="s" />: <InlineMath math="G = H" />

#### Translating Statements

1.  **Statement 1**: <InlineMath math="A = B" /> or <InlineMath math="C \neq D" />

    <MathContainer>
      <BlockMath math="p \lor q" />
    </MathContainer>

    This form is equivalent to the implication: <InlineMath math="\sim p \to q" />.

2.  **Statement 2**: If <InlineMath math="E = F" /> then <InlineMath math="G = H" />

    <MathContainer>
      <BlockMath math="r \to s" />
    </MathContainer>

    This form is equivalent to the contrapositive: <InlineMath math="\sim s \to \sim r" />.

3.  **Statement 3**: <InlineMath math="C = D" /> or <InlineMath math="G \neq H" />

    Note that <InlineMath math="C = D" /> is the negation of <InlineMath math="q" /> (<InlineMath math="\sim q" />), and <InlineMath math="G \neq H" /> is the negation of <InlineMath math="s" /> (<InlineMath math="\sim s" />).

    <MathContainer>
      <BlockMath math="\sim q \lor \sim s" />
    </MathContainer>

    This form is equivalent to the implication: <InlineMath math="q \to \sim s" />.

#### Drawing Conclusion

We have the following logical chain:
1.  From Statement 1: <InlineMath math="\sim p \to q" />
2.  From Statement 3: <InlineMath math="q \to \sim s" />
3.  From Statement 2: <InlineMath math="\sim s \to \sim r" />

Using syllogism, we can combine all three:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="(\sim p \to q) \land (q \to \sim s) \land (\sim s \to \sim r) \implies \sim p \to \sim r" />
</MathContainer>

The final result is <InlineMath math="\sim p \to \sim r" />. This implication form is equivalent to a disjunction (or):

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\sim p \to \sim r \equiv p \lor \sim r" />
</MathContainer>

#### Translating Back to Sentences

*   <InlineMath math="p" /> is <InlineMath math="A = B" />
*   <InlineMath math="\sim r" /> is <InlineMath math="E \neq F" />

So the conclusion is: **<InlineMath math="A = B" /> or <InlineMath math="E \neq F" />**.
