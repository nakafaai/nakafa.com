Let's translate the statements into mathematical logic.

Let:

*   <InlineMath math="p" />: Nakafa students work on conditional empathy questions.
*   <InlineMath math="q" />: Nakafa students can work on TOBK questions in the Nakafa application.
*   <InlineMath math="r" />: Nakafa students get Learning Achievement Evaluation (EPB) results.

The given statements are:

1.  If Nakafa students work on conditional empathy questions, then they can work on TOBK questions in the Nakafa application (<InlineMath math="p \rightarrow q" />).
2.  If Nakafa students work on TOBK questions in the Nakafa application, then they get Learning Achievement Evaluation (EPB) results (<InlineMath math="q \rightarrow r" />).
3.  Dito does not get Learning Achievement Evaluation (EPB) results (<InlineMath math="\sim r" />).

From premises 1 and 2, we can use syllogism:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\begin{align*} & p \rightarrow q \\ & q \rightarrow r \\ \hline \therefore \ & p \rightarrow r \end{align*}" />
</MathContainer>

Next, we use that conclusion (<InlineMath math="p \rightarrow r" />) with premise 3 (<InlineMath math="\sim r" />) using the Modus Tollens rule:

<MathContainer>
  <BlockMath math="\begin{align*} & p \rightarrow r \\ & \sim r \\ \hline \therefore \ & \sim p \end{align*}" />
</MathContainer>

The conclusion is <InlineMath math="\sim p" />, which means **Dito does not work on conditional empathy questions**.
